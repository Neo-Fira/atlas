# Atlas MVP Definition

## MVP Goal

**Version 1.0 exists to give a first-time traveler a trustworthy, complete plan for reaching and experiencing the Fann Mountains corridor—especially Seven Lakes and Iskanderkul—from Samarkand, Panjakent, or Dushanbe.**

The MVP succeeds if a traveler can arrive from Google with a specific question, determine whether the journey fits them, understand the real logistics and trade-offs, prepare responsibly, and either create a viable travel plan or leave Atlas to book an appropriate option with clear expectations.

Version 1.0 is not a broad “Tajikistan tourism website.” It is the clearest answer to a concentrated set of high-intent, high-uncertainty travel decisions. Depth on this corridor is more valuable than shallow national coverage.

## Core User

The core user is a first-time, independent-minded international traveler planning a short Tajikistan experience as part of a visit to Uzbekistan or a wider Central Asia journey.

They are often starting in Samarkand, or considering Dushanbe as a base. They are drawn to the Seven Lakes, Iskanderkul, Panjakent, and the Fann Mountains, but are uncertain about border crossing, transport, cost, road conditions, physical effort, time, language, and whether a guided service is necessary. They want meaningful travel, not a generic tour package; they will use a service when it is the sensible choice.

The MVP must also work for a traveler who is already in Samarkand, Panjakent, or Dushanbe and needs a decision quickly. It must not assume unlimited internet, unlimited time, or previous knowledge of Tajikistan.

## Core User Journey

### 1. Enter from Google with a real question

The traveler searches for a query such as “Seven Lakes from Samarkand,” “Iskanderkul day trip,” “Samarkand to Tajikistan border,” or “Seven Lakes vs Iskanderkul.” They land directly on the page that answers that question, not on a generic home page or a tour catalog.

### 2. Decide whether the journey fits

The landing page gives a concise answer first: what the journey is, who it suits, who should reconsider, total time and effort, expected cost range, seasonality, the major constraints, and the confidence/source date for practical facts. It links to the next decision only when useful.

### 3. Build a viable plan

The traveler follows the natural sequence: destination → route → border or transport guidance → comparison or itinerary → preparation checklist. They can choose a one-day, two-day, weekend, or seven-day plan and see the assumptions, dependencies, and alternatives.

### 4. Choose how to act

Atlas supports two equally valid outcomes:

- **Create and save a self-directed plan.** The traveler saves the route, itinerary, and preparation brief for later use.
- **Leave to book a service.** Where Atlas has verified enough material information, it presents clearly labeled options or a direct booking handoff. The traveler understands the provider, the price basis, inclusions, exclusions, terms, and what “request” or “confirmed” means before leaving Atlas.

Atlas does not require a booking to count the journey as successful. Its responsibility is a well-informed decision and a workable plan.

### 5. Use the plan in the real world

The saved or shareable trip brief contains essential route steps, preparation items, material assumptions, and fallback links. If a relevant condition becomes uncertain or changes, Atlas states that clearly and offers the safest practical next action.

### MVP feature boundaries

- The MVP has **one planning corridor**, not a nationwide route finder.
- A saved plan is a concise trip brief, not a full personal travel-management system.
- Booking handoff is informational and transparent, not an Atlas checkout or inventory marketplace.
- Current-condition notices are limited to MVP routes and must never imply continuous real-time coverage.

## Features NOT Included

| Postponed feature | Why it is not in Version 1.0 |
| --- | --- |
| **Atlas booking checkout** | It would shift focus from trustworthy planning to transaction operations before Atlas has earned the trust and content depth required. |
| **Open marketplace or operator directory** | A directory without a mature evaluation standard, review policy, and issue process would create false authority and commercial pressure. |
| **User reviews and public ratings** | Reviews require verification, moderation, and a clear distinction between firsthand experience and fact. Volume is not a substitute for trust. |
| **Nationwide destination coverage** | Broad but thin coverage would repeat the market’s existing failure: inspiration without decision-ready information. |
| **Pamir Highway, Wakhan Valley, and nationwide itineraries** | These deserve their own source-checked route and condition model; adding them early would dilute the Fann Mountains corridor. |
| **Personalized recommendations** | Atlas must first establish a trustworthy editorial baseline before tailoring advice to individuals. |
| **Live transport, weather, or availability claims** | These require sustained verification discipline. The MVP can state dated conditions and uncertainty, but must not simulate real-time certainty. |
| **Community forums, messaging, or social feeds** | They create moderation, safety, and misinformation obligations that do not advance the core job of planning well. |
| **Loyalty, discounts, deals, and urgency mechanics** | They optimize conversion rather than judgment and conflict with Atlas’s trust-first position. |
| **Accommodation, restaurant, and attraction databases** | They create a large, maintenance-heavy inventory problem before Atlas proves its differentiated planning value. |
| **Multiple languages beyond the launch editorial language** | Careful translation and local nuance are essential; expansion should follow a proven, maintainable content standard rather than machine volume. |

## Success Metrics

The MVP is successful when it demonstrably improves the traveler’s decision quality. Metrics must be reviewed alongside qualitative evidence; no traffic or conversion metric can overrule a trust failure.

### Traveler outcomes

- A meaningful share of surveyed users report that Atlas helped them decide whether the core journey was right for them.
- Users can correctly identify the key constraints, preparation needs, and uncertainty in their chosen route after using Atlas.
- Users report arriving more prepared and encountering fewer avoidable surprises about time, cost, effort, border process, or transport.
- Users who face a change of plan can find a relevant alternative or next action without needing to restart their research.
- Users save or share a trip brief because it is useful, not because a gate forces them to do so.

### Product signals

- Search visitors reach a relevant route, destination, comparison, or guide page and continue to the next logical planning decision.
- The core path—destination → route → preparation or itinerary—has strong completion relative to entry volume.
- A meaningful share of core-route visitors save a trip brief, follow a clearly labeled booking handoff, or report that they can now proceed independently.
- Correction reports are acknowledged, resolved, and visibly reflected in the affected guidance.
- No recurring pattern emerges of users misunderstanding price units, booking status, service responsibility, or material route constraints.

### Trust guardrails

The MVP is not successful if it grows through misleading traffic, hidden commercial influence, stale practical guidance, or unexplained errors. A smaller audience that uses Atlas confidently and recommends it for accuracy is a stronger result than large traffic with poor preparedness.

## Risks

| Risk | Why it matters | MVP response |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Information becomes outdated** | Border rules, road conditions, prices, and access can change quickly; stale certainty can cause real harm. | Date and source material facts, define review ownership, state uncertainty, and provide official-source paths where appropriate. |
| **Scope expands before depth is proven** | Adding destinations, tours, or features can make Atlas generic and weaken maintenance quality. | Keep the fixed 33-page corridor launch set; additions require a demonstrated user problem and the Information Architecture quality gates. |
| **Commercial pressure compromises independence** | Operator relationships can quietly distort recommendations. | Keep booking external, label every relationship, show alternatives, and do not sell ranking or editorial influence. |
| **The content is useful but not discoverable** | High-quality guidance cannot help travelers who cannot find it. | Center launch pages on exact search intent and connect them through the route-to-readiness journey. |
| **Travelers mistake Atlas for an official or real-time authority** | This creates unsafe reliance. | Clearly identify sources, verification dates, confidence, scope, and when a traveler must confirm directly. |
| **Local representation is shallow or extractive** | Atlas would fail its mission even if the planning content performs. | Use attributable local knowledge, contextualize culture, avoid stereotypes, and publish responsible-visiting guidance. |
| **The journey is too complex for a concise product** | Overly long content can overwhelm the same traveler Atlas aims to help. | Put the immediate answer first; reveal detail only when it supports the next decision; maintain a concise trip brief. |

## Future Expansion

Expansion begins only after the MVP demonstrates useful, accurate planning outcomes and a sustainable content-maintenance practice.

1. **Deepen the Fann Mountains corridor.** Add more destination, route, experience, and seasonal pages only where user questions and verified local knowledge justify them.
2. **Extend the model to the Pamirs.** Apply the same decision-complete system to the Pamir Highway and Wakhan Valley, beginning with high-uncertainty routes and source-dated conditions rather than broad attraction lists.
3. **Build accountable service profiles.** Introduce a small, governed set of operators, guides, stays, and drivers with transparent evaluation criteria, material terms, and an issue path.
4. **Strengthen in-trip utility.** Expand route-condition notices, alternatives, and saved-trip updates only where Atlas can maintain clear verification and scope.
5. **Add verified firsthand perspective.** Introduce carefully moderated traveler and local contributions that add useful context without replacing factual guidance.
6. **Broaden trip planning.** Add more starting points, durations, family and accessibility paths, and country-wide itineraries once their dependencies are trustworthy.
7. **Consider deeper commercial support last.** Any booking capability must prove that it strengthens traveler understanding, transparency, and recourse rather than weakening Atlas’s independent judgment.

## Decision Rule

When evaluating an MVP addition, ask: **Would this make a first-time traveler materially more prepared to take a real journey through the Fann Mountains corridor?** If the answer is not clearly yes, it does not belong in Version 1.0.
